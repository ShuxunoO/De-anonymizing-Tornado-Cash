With the widespread adoption of blockchain technology, cryptocurrencies have become an important part of the global financial system. At the same time, the decentralization, pseudonymity, and cross-border liquidity of blockchain also make it a hotspot for laundering illicit funds. As one of the most prominent mixing protocols in the Ethereum ecosystem, Tornado Cash (TC) uses zero-knowledge proofs to break the on-chain linkage between deposit and withdrawal addresses, which has also made it widely used for laundering proceeds from hacks and other crimes on blockchain. Existing on-chain forensic studies mainly rely on shallow heuristic features, such as gas fees, temporal proximity, amount similarity, and Ethereum Name ervice (ENS) ownership. These methods heavily depend on subjective assumptions and expert intuition, which limits the reliability of their results. Meanwhile, the lack of publicly available, high-quality labeled datasets has further hindered research on TC deposit-withdrawal address linkage.
In this paper, to address these challenges, we propose a multidimensional transaction-feature-based detection method for identifying linkage between Tornado Cash deposit and withdrawal addresses. By leveraging the historical transaction patterns of key entities involved in the Tornado Cash protocol, including deposit addresses, recipient addresses, and withdrawal initiators, we design three linkage patterns: direct linkage, gas-funding linkage, and transaction-intensity linkage. Using this method, we identify 10,831 high-confidence Tornado Cash deposit-withdrawal address pairs and construct and release the Tornado Cash Linkage dataset. Based on this dataset, we conduct a comprehensive empirical analysis of user deposit-withdrawal behavioral patterns, inter-transaction time-gap distributions, and the structural characteristics of linked address interactions, demonstrating the practical value of our method and dataset for real-world Tornado Cash-related investigation tasks. Our work provides a valuable data foundation and methodological support for mixing-transaction analysis, anonymity evaluation, and illicit fund tracing.
